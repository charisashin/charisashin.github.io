Journey:

AI-Assisted Journaling

Overview

AI applications can offer many valuable qualities to mental health treatment, including 24/7 accessibility and responsiveness, privacy protection through anonymity, and potential solutions to barriers such as a lack of providers in rural areas and feelings of fear, guilt, or shame that may stop someone with depression from reaching out to others.

However, current AI-based technologies revolve around creating patterned and consistent Q&A chatbots and do not employ the adaptive and personalized qualities that would help a user actively learn from their own journaling. This UCLA HCI Lab project aims to use expressive journaling to aid a user in changing their negative thinking.

The Goal

Working with psychology professors and counselors, our team from UCLA's HCI Lab aimed to explore how AI could aid in mental health intervention, specifically through Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT). Through developing Journey, an emotional support platform in the form of an interactive web journal, we are actively seeking ways to help users learn from thoughts relating to feelings of depression, anxiety, or stress.

Existing Products

There are two primary types of mental health technologies on the market right now: self-help reflection platforms without an interactive AI (check my case study on one of them here!), and AI-based chatbots that help a user step through their thoughts using patterned answers.

Insights

  1. Varying levels of personal connection.
    Applications can range from intimate communication through AI-led text message conversations to more impersonal, self-guided documentation.
  2. The necessity of raising emotional awareness.
    All of the programs focus on helping the user to raise their own awareness of their emotional state and reactions.
  3. Limited interaction and personalization.
    Most applications have a fixed level of AI intervention, which doesn't allow for users to take control of the expression process.

Professional Feedback

To ensure that this web application is clincally viable and to evaluate the quality of our interactive system, our team met regularly with psychology professionals, including professors and practicing counselors from UCLA. Regular feedback from professionals, as listed below, provided insights for each iteration of both the back-end system and front-end interface design. I regularly drew from notes from the interviews to inform my UX decisions.

Insights

  1. Technology as an emerging means for therapy.
    Research indicates that people will be more honest with a computer than with a clinician or therapist they don't know.
  2. Simple language is more accessible.
    It's important to make the language of the application as simple as possible, since "over-intellectualization" can actually have negative effects on the user's experience.
  3. Evaluating interventions.
    In principle, every assessment is an intervention and every intervention is an assessment. Thus, every time a user doesn't respond, it's informative of something deeper.

AI Intervention

Mapping the App Features

To understand how the AI would interact with the user through the journaling platform, our group devised a chart to display the different levels of AI intervention and what features would correlate with each tier.

Features Hierarchy

The app incorporates four main features: Auto-Suggest, Reminders, Guiding Dialogue, and Connection & Friendship. Each feature ties in with a unique objective to ultimately guide the user in reorienting their thought patterns.







Motion Demo

After garnering information and feedback from our interviews with professors and psychology professionals, my co-designer, Brandon Ngo, established the UI design system and language for the second design iteration, while I covered motion design details for seamless interactions.



Feature 1: Auto-Suggest

With the goal of encouraging users to change their mindsets, we provided alternative words and phrases to what they wrote down. Similar to the smart compose function of Gmail, the application generates more positive ways to finish sentences.

Feature 2: Reminders

Reminders help improve the user's awareness of their emotional status and identify distortions on their own. A familiar dictionary definition design helps the user commit certain distortions to memory to avoid in the future.

Feature 3: Guiding Dialogue

To encourage further introspection, we provided users with probing questions in order to discover the deeper reason behind the their negative emotions. We chose to use a moving three-dot symbol to represent the computer "typing" a response to the entry, much like in a text conversation with a human companion.

Feature 4: Connection & Friendship

In order to help the user build a collaborative relationship with the AI, empathetic phrasing and a gentle UI design were critical. Softer elements make the user feel more at ease and more comfortable with sharing deeply.

Enhancing accessibility

Because mental health affects anyone regardless of background or ability, accessibility measures are a critical consideration. For Journey, I led the initiative in implementing inclusive design choices for the interface, which comprised of creating color manipulations according to W3C's Web Content Accessibility Guidelines and selective portions of Apple's Human Interface Guidelines.






Dark mode

To address the problems of eye strain and to improve visibility for users with low vision and those sensitive to bright light, I spearheaded the development of a dark mode option initiated by a toggle switch. By designing inclusively for those with vision needs, I could also cater to a wider user base with temporary impairments (like being in a dark room) or those who may just prefer a darker interface.


Example of a dark mode page









Accessible color contrasts

To accomodate colorblind or color-sensitive users, I followed WGAC 2.0 standards and developed a color palette that accounted for both deuteranopia and protanopia colorblindness conditions. Such a palette could be applied through the Themes tab.

Takeaways & Next Steps

Working on Journey truly challenged me as a designer and researcher, especially in the realm of accessibility. To fully empathize with a range of users, I had to carefully adhere to inclusive design standards and focus on solving for a certain user group first. I was inspired to brainstorm further about how I might approach building solutions for mental health problems in a way that would allow for anyone to participate in the experience with a sense of belonging.

Moving Forward

Journey is still a developmental work in progress. Next steps involve moving into initiating a pilot program with real-time and constant users. I will be working to actively carry out usability tests on a larger demographic to improve the last iteration.
